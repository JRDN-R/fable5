! The Issue

Your AC can't hold setpoint (71°F set, 76°F actual), and the ceiling temperature map shows why in plain sight: the heat gain is not uniform. You have distinct hot zones — Master Bath / WIC / Aro's Bedroom running +80°F at the ceiling, and Master Bedroom / Dining at ~76°F — while other rooms (Kitchen/Breakfast, Ronin's, rear Bedroom) sit comfortably at ~72°F.

If the HVAC system were the problem, the whole house would be uniformly warm. A patchy, zone-specific ceiling temperature pattern like this points up, at the attic, not at the equipment.

⚡ What's Causing It

Primary cause: degraded attic insulation under a ~140°F attic.

  • Your insulation is 19 years old, settled/disturbed, averaging ~7.4 inches of blown fiberglass ≈ R-17 to R-19 — less than half the R-38 minimum recommended for a Florida attic.
  • Your thin spots (6 inches ≈ R-13–15) and displaced/rolled-back batts almost certainly sit directly above your red zones. That +80°F ceiling in Aro's room and the master bath is a 140°F attic radiating through an inadequate thermal barrier straight into the living space. That's also why Aro's room reads ~82°F while Ronin's reads ~73°F — same AC, different ceiling.
  • Every duct run in that 140°F attic is also absorbing heat before the air reaches the vents (87°F duct surfaces), shaving capacity off every supply run.

Secondary/contributing factors:

  • Possibly blocked soffit vents (you noted "haven't checked") — restricted intake ventilation drives attic temps higher.
  • The small duct leak (75–78°F cool spot) — minor, but it's conditioned air dumping into the attic.
  • Setpoint expectation: 71°F on a 95°F+, 75–80% humidity day is beyond what almost any correctly sized residential system is designed to hold. Systems are sized to maintain ~75°F at design conditions. Some of your 5°F gap is envelope failure; a degree or two of it is physics.

✓ What Is NOT the Problem

Your HVAC is doing its job, and the data proves it:

❄️~20°F supply/return split (71–73°F return, 50–54°F supply) — right in spec
💧Condensate line constantly draining — it's pulling humidity hard
📐Correctly sized: 3 tons for 1,760 sq ft (~587 sq ft/ton) is appropriate for Florida
🔧2 years old, clean filter, running full capacity
Don't get upsold Do not let anyone sell you a bigger system. The AC is winning a fight it shouldn't have to fight — it's cooling the house and the heat pouring through an R-17 ceiling.

→ What To Do (In Order)

  1. Air-seal the ceiling plane first. While the attic is accessible (before adding insulation), seal penetrations — wire holes, plumbing chases, top plates, around light fixtures/bath fans — with foam/caulk. Cheap, high impact, impossible after new insulation goes in.
  2. Seal the duct leak with mastic (your existing plan — correct).
  3. Verify soffit vents are open; install baffles at the eaves so new insulation doesn't block them.
  4. Reposition the displaced batts flat and tight over the red zones (Master Bath, WIC, Aro's room). This is free and targets your worst areas immediately.
  5. Top up the whole attic to R-38+ (~13–16" total depth). Either blown fiberglass over existing (easiest — rental blowers are often free with material purchase at Lowe's/Home Depot) or unfaced R-30 batts laid perpendicular over the existing blown-in. Prioritize the red-zone areas with extra depth. Your existing insulation is dry, mold-free, pest-free — it stays; you're just burying it under more.
  6. After the insulation is fixed: re-enable stage 1 on the heat pump. Right now stage-2-only brute force is compensating for the envelope. Once the attic is fixed, stage 1 will handle most days with longer, quieter, more efficient runs and better dehumidification.

★ Expected Result

The payoff With R-38+ overhead, the ceiling temperature map should flatten toward uniform ~72–74°F, Aro's room should match Ronin's, the system will cycle instead of running flat-out, and holding low-70s setpoints on hot days becomes realistic. This is roughly a $600–1,000 DIY fix solving what looks like a multi-thousand-dollar "AC problem."
